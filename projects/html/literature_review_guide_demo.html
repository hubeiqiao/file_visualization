Glossary

Term Definition
Boolean operators Logical connectors (AND, OR, NOT) used in database searches to refine search results by including or excluding specific terms.
Grey literature Information produced outside of traditional publishing and distribution channels, such as reports, conference proceedings, and government documents.
Human oversight The involvement of human judgment and decision-making in monitoring, guiding, or intervening in automated systems to ensure ethical, accurate, and responsible outcomes.
PRISMA-ScR The Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses for Scoping Reviews, a framework that provides guidelines for conducting and reporting scoping reviews.
Scoping review A type of literature review that maps key concepts, evidence, and research gaps in a field, typically without assessing the quality of the included studies.
Systematic review A structured and comprehensive review of existing research on a specific question, using a rigorous methodology to identify, appraise, and synthesize relevant studies.

Introduction

What is a Scoping Review?

A scoping review gathers and examines a wide range of sources to show what is known and unknown. Gaps in the literature can inspire new business ideas or research questions.

Within Technology Innovation Management (TIM), scoping reviews can inform:

Venture pitches
Market analysis
Business opportunities
Product development
Process improvement
Innovation ecosystems

In Technology Innovation Management, a scoping review helps you see what is already known—whether in blockchain commercialization, open innovation ecosystems, or emerging AI startups—and spot new opportunities. By combining human expertise with AI tools, TIM researchers can sift through large, varied studies, uncover practical insights from non-traditional sources, and guide innovation strategies for the future.

Unlike a systematic review, scoping reviews cast a wider net, enabling TIM practitioners to gain insights into rapidly evolving technology landscapes—sometimes well before methodologies and theoretical constructs have fully matured.

1.1 When to Use a Scoping Review

In TIM, scoping reviews are especially valuable when:

Broad or Exploratory Questions

Use a scoping review if you wish to explore a wide variety of studies on a topic, rather than measuring how well something works.

Example:

Investigating how AI-driven platforms are transforming technology commercialization across various industries.

Emerging Topics

Use when the field is new, and key concepts, definitions, and knowledge gaps are unclear.

Example:

Mapping quantum computing applications in early-stage technology startups, where key players, definitions, and standard practices are still evolving.

Heterogeneous Literature

Use when studies cover diverse methodologies, study designs, and disciplines, making systematic synthesis impractical.

Example:

Integrating findings from multidisciplinary sources (engineering, business, sociology, etc.) on the use of AI in different industries.

1.2 Key Frameworks

Scoping reviews in TIM often follow one or more of five main frameworks:

Arksey and O'Malley (2005)

Best for: Fast-changing fields

Five-step outline that you can update as you go. Works well for fast-changing fields like financial technology (fintech).

Example:

Mapping wearable technology for remote patient monitoring, identifying emerging sensor innovations and untapped market niches.

Levac et al. (2010)

Best for: Stakeholder engagement

Extends Arksey and O'Malley's model by stressing stakeholder engagement—vital for TIM researchers collaborating with startups.

Example:

Engaging accelerators, manufacturers, and regulators in refining a review on IoT-based smart manufacturing.

JBI Framework

Best for: Methodological rigor

Offers a refined methodology for planning and conducting scoping reviews with enhanced rigor and transparency.

Example:

Conducting a structured review on 3D-printing technologies for rapid prototyping to boost credibility with investors.

1.3 Benefits of Using AI for Scoping Reviews

AI can manage large volumes of diverse data—quickly and consistently—making it invaluable for scoping reviews in TIM. It helps teams move faster to market, discover better product-market fits, stay ahead of disruptive technologies, and uncover new opportunities or threats.

Speed & Efficiency

  • • Rapid literature screening: AI quickly scans large volumes of articles, patents, and reports.
  • • Automated deduplication: AI removes duplicate studies, saving considerable time.

Enhanced Discovery

  • • Diverse data sources: AI pulls from journals, preprints, websites, and more.
  • • Intelligent recommendations: ML surfaces relevant but less visible research.

Improved Organization

  • • Topic clustering: AI groups articles into thematic clusters for quicker navigation.
  • • Automatic tagging: Keywords and metadata extraction for better organization.

Key Takeaway: AI speeds up the research process and delivers more actionable insights. However, human oversight remains essential to validate AI-generated findings, detect biases, and ensure the accuracy of synthesized information.

1. Formulate the Review Question and Scope

A scoping review requires a broad and exploratory review question that captures the full range of relevant studies and perspectives.

AI's Role

  • • Generate review question variations and refine clarity
  • • Suggest related topics, synonyms, and search terms
  • • Analyze existing literature to highlight research gaps
  • • Identify key authors and influential papers

Human Oversight

  • • Ensure clarity and feasibility of review questions
  • • Validate AI-generated refinements for focus and precision
  • • Incorporate relevant academic models and theoretical frameworks

Guidance

  • • Compare AI-suggested questions with published scoping reviews
  • • Align the review question with existing frameworks (e.g., PCC: Population, Concept, Context)
  • • Use AI-generated starting points, then refine with domain expertise

PCC Framework for Structuring Review Questions

Population

Who or what is being studied?

Concept

What are the core ideas being examined?

Context

What's the setting or environment?

Example: Product Development
  • Population: Startups developing AI-powered products
  • Concept: Challenges in scaling AI-driven prototypes
  • Context: Venture acceleration programs

Review question: What are the key challenges and success factors influencing the scaling of AI-driven product prototypes in technology startups within venture acceleration programs?

Example: Digital Transformation
  • Population: Mid-sized manufacturing firms
  • Concept: Adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies
  • Context: North American manufacturing

Review question: What are the primary barriers and facilitators influencing the adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies in mid-sized Canadian manufacturing firms?

Step 1 Outputs

Research Question

A clearly defined, focused question using PCC or another appropriate framework.

Review Objectives

Concise statement outlining what the review aims to achieve and knowledge gaps to address.

Preliminary Keywords

Initial set of keywords, Boolean operators, and alternate terms for database searches.
