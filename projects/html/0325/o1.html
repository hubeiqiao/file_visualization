PART 1. FOUNDATION OF SCOPING REVIEWS

Term Definition
Boolean operators Logical connectors (AND, OR, NOT) used in database searches to refine search results by including or excluding specific terms.
Grey literature Information produced outside traditional publishing and distribution channels, such as reports, conference proceedings, and government documents.
Human oversight The involvement of human judgment and decision-making in monitoring, guiding, or intervening in automated systems to ensure ethical, accurate, and responsible outcomes.
PRISMA-ScR The Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta- Analyses for Scoping Reviews, a framework providing guidelines for conducting and reporting scoping reviews.
Scoping review A type of literature review that maps key concepts, evidence, and research gaps in a field, typically without assessing the quality of the included studies.
Systematic review A structured and comprehensive review of existing research on a specific question, using a rigorous methodology to identify, appraise, and synthesize relevant studies.

A scoping review gathers and examines a wide range of sources to show what is known and unknown. In Technology Innovation Management (TIM), a scoping review helps you see what is already known and spot new opportunities. By combining human expertise with AI tools, TIM researchers can sift through large, varied studies, uncover practical insights from non-traditional sources, and guide innovation strategies for the future.

Unlike a systematic review, scoping reviews cast a wider net, enabling TIM students, faculty, and practitioners to gain insights into rapidly evolving technology landscapes. If your review question is narrowly defined and aims to evaluate quality or effectiveness, a systematic review may be more suitable.

In TIM, scoping reviews are especially valuable for broad or exploratory questions, emerging topics, heterogeneous literature, minimal need for critical appraisal, and identifying gaps and trends. Use a scoping review when:

  • Review questions are broad or exploratory
  • Emerging topics
  • Heterogeneous literature
  • Minimal need for critical appraisal
  • Identifying gaps and trends

Scoping reviews in TIM often follow one or more frameworks: Arksey and O’Malley (2005), Levac et al. (2010), Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI), PRISMA-ScR, and Recent AI-focused updates. Each offers strengths for mapping emerging topics, emphasizing stakeholder engagement, or ensuring standardized reporting.

  • Speed and efficiency: Rapid screening, automated deduplication.
  • Enhanced discovery: Diverse data sources, intelligent recommendations.
  • Improved organization: Topic clustering, automatic tagging.
  • Scalable analysis: Big data handling, dynamic updating.
  • Reduced human error: Consistent screening, refined accuracy over time.
  • Insight generation: Text summarization, trend detection.
  • Cost savings: Streamlined workflow, resource reallocation.

While scoping reviews offer broad overviews, they come with drawbacks: lack of critical appraisal, broad scope, no formal strength-of-evidence assessment, selection bias, and more. Time and resource demands can be high, and definitions of scope can be tricky in fast-moving tech fields.

AI tools can facilitate tasks but may introduce mistakes (“hallucinations”), lack contextual understanding, or reflect algorithmic biases. Overreliance on AI can overlook foundational works or grey literature. Mitigation involves cross-checking references, adding human oversight, and ensuring ethical guidelines are upheld.

When using AI for scoping reviews in TIM, remember that different biases can shape your understanding of technology trends. These include selection bias, algorithmic bias, confirmation bias, citation bias, language and accessibility bias, and data hallucination. Mitigation strategies involve extending searches, verifying references, and balancing results with human judgment.

In TIM scoping reviews, human oversight is essential for accuracy and contextual understanding, evaluating the suitability and credibility of included studies, and intervening when AI misinterprets key concepts.

Grey literature can provide real-world insights. Steps include defining scope, picking keywords, selecting relevant sources (government databases, think tanks, industry reports, patent databases), searching specialized databases, contacting organizations and experts, and filtering/evaluating sources for authority and credibility.

Organizing a scoping review in TIM involves many sources. Effective reference management uses tools like Zotero, Mendeley, or EndNote for efficient organization, automated citation formatting, and collaboration. These tools also help detect duplicates and integrate with AI-based tools for advanced literature mapping.

PART 2. METHOD TO PRODUCE SCOPING REVIEWS

This method is tailored for TIM contexts, integrating AI tools for enhanced efficiency. Each step includes AI’s role, human oversight, and guidance for best practices.

1

Step 1: Formulate the Review Question and Scope

Define the broad but structured review question. Use frameworks like PCC or CIMO, incorporate AI for refining search terms, and validate scope with human oversight.

2

Step 2: Search for Articles

Develop a search strategy, use AI tools (ChatGPT, Elicit, etc.) for advanced queries, adapt search for multiple databases, and store in reference managers.

3

Step 3: Select Articles

Employ a two-step screening process (title/abstract, then full-text). Leverage AI for classification and Rayyan AI for screening, but apply dual human screening for reliability.

4

Step 4: Extract Data

Use AI to assist in summarizing and organizing key information (author, year, findings, etc.). Validate with manual oversight to avoid misinterpretations.

5

Step 5: Analyze and Synthesize Data

Summarize key themes, categorize studies, generate thematic maps, and verify AI-generated outputs with human evaluation.

6

Step 6: Interpret Results

Draw meaningful insights, highlight knowledge gaps, and ensure logical coherence. AI suggestions are helpful, but human expertise is crucial for depth and nuance.

7

Step 7: Write the Scoping Review

Generate initial drafts with AI, refine for academic rigor, and fact-check citations. Use PRISMA-ScR and JBI for structure.

8

Step 8: Incorporate Ethical Considerations

Address transparency of AI use, mitigate bias, ensure privacy, follow institutional research ethics, and maintain a balanced perspective.

9

Step 9: Disseminate Findings

Publish results, create policy briefs, pitch presentations, and share open access data. Use AI to draft summaries for multiple audiences, with human oversight for final polishing.

Checklist for Authors and Reviewers

1. Formulate the research question and scope

  • PCC framework usage, specificity & relevance for TIM.
  • Well-defined scope (time, literature types, geography).

2. Identify and select relevant studies

  • Clear search strategy using multiple databases & AI tools.

3. Screen and select studies using Rayyan AI

  • Dual screening for consistency, tagging, categorization.

4. Extract and synthesize data

  • Key study characteristics, innovation drivers, barriers.

5. Visualize and report insights

  • Concept maps, AI-generated summaries, citation networks.

6. Translate research into action

  • Product dev. opportunities, tech adoption strategies, competitor analysis.

7. Disseminate findings

  • Research vignettes, pitch presentations, policy briefs.

8. Ethical Considerations in AI for TIM

  • Bias mitigation, transparency, privacy, and accountability.
